Structural Work

Within the context of a built environment, structural work refers to each component that forms part of a building's external walls or roof. This includes anything that is constructed or assembled from interrelated parts that are fixed at a particular location on the ground.

Primarily, structural work refers to buildings, but it can also apply to any structural body that is designed to bear loads, even if its intention is not to be lived in by people. In engineered plans, you will usually find these buildings (like tunnels and bridges) listed under "non-building" structural work.

Shoddy structural work is usually the cause for the unwanted movement in buildings, which generally produces a considerable deal of worry for homeowners. Failure of building materials, physical disturbance, and subsidence are just some of the factors that will require structural repairs, and affect the structural integrity of a property.

Fortunately, there are a few reasons why a building can move, and the modern structural repair technology we have here at Arise Constructions means that we're able to provide you with efficient solutions to tackle any structural work issue you may have at your property.

How to Identify Structural Damages?

Ideally, no one wants to deal with structural repairs or reinforcing structures. However, whether you live in a home built 50 years ago or very recently, it's possible that a reinforcing structure situation may occur. Whether its cause comes from poor construction, a natural disaster, poor planning, or just everyday entropy, you may require structural repairs sooner than anticipated.

The good news? Well, it's possible to catch structural damage early -you just need to know what to look out for:

  • Cracks in Brick and Stonework - Finding cracks in the brick and stonework of your home is a prevalent sign of house settling issues. They tend to form as the home begins to settle more on one side than the other. As the settling continues to exert more force on the house, you may find stonework and brick cracks.

  • Porch Pulling Away From Home - Another common exterior sign - a porch pulling away. If you do notice this, ensure you don't dismiss it as an annoyance -it could be signs of house settling issues that will strain your wallet even more in the future.

  • Gaps in Window and Door Frames -If you start to look around your windows and doors and notice small openings in the seams, you may be experiencing some form of warping. This structural repair issue may be down to the fact that the home didn't experience the right kind of preparation - which could ultimately lead to a foundational concern.

  • Nail Pops -Nail pops appear when the nail behind your drywall pushes itself through. Not only can this be unsightly, but it can also create prominent cracks and slight bulges on your wall. Nail pops usually occur due to house settling issues.

  • Cabinets That Swing Open Alone -Although this may seem paranormal, there's no need to get your phone out and start recording just yet. It could simply just be down to doors having a more challenging time staying shut as the home settles and cabinets tilt from the wall.

  • Uneven Floors -If you have noticed slightly tilted floors, it again, could be down to home settling issues. Uneven floors occur when your house settles unevenly on one side -leading the structure.

If you happen to notice any of the above defects, don't panic. Feel free to reach out and give us a call -our licensed engineers here at Arise Constructions in Denver only use highly versatile materials to ensure structural integrity that will last for years to come.

Engineered Plans

An engineering plan refers to the plans and specifications for structural work to be completed or purchased within any subdistrict. This includes profiles, maps, procedures, and any other data necessary to set the path for the location, character of work, the property benefited, and whether it's damaged or taken.

Engineered plans are essential - not just for the safety of the people living in the building -but are also highly necessary when it comes to satisfying building codes.

When are Structural Engineered Plans Needed?

Structural engineered plans are required when:

  • Constructing residential projects like houses and apartments.
  • Rebuilding a residence that large storms have damaged.
  • Making changes to your doors and windows
  • Chimney renovations
  • Home extensions
  • Installing solar panels
  • Taking out or modifying an inside wall
  • Re-working the structure of a building due to flaws in the original building process

Even if you are just reinforcing structures, you may require a structural engineer to inspect your property. If the engineer finds issues or safety concerns within the walls, roof, or floors upon inspection, it will require an engineered plan.

Structural Repairs

Structural repairs refer to any repairs to the structure of a building. It includes the building foundation, wall, roof, and columns. In the construction industry, structural repairs are a technical term, contrasted to renovations. Primarily, they are changes made to a property in order to bring it up to local health and safety standards. However, unlike renovations, they don't add a lot of value to the property itself.

Buildings usually require structural repairs due to their age, deterioration, or some unavoidable environmental factors like subsidence or water damage.

Reinforcing Structure

Reinforcing structure refers to the process of upgrading the property's structural system. This procedure is usually carried out to improve a building's performance under existing loads, or to increase its strength to carry additional loads.

The need for reinforcing structure is commonly caused by building codes, change in use, deficiencies, or deterioration caused by construction and design errors.

Vaulted Ceilings and Raising Ceilings

When it comes to controversial architectural elements, vaulted ceilings and raising ceilings are some of the most divisive, with many praising them for their grand, lofty flair. Vaulted ceilings and raising ceilings have been around for centuries, and it's highly likely that they aren't going anywhere.

Speaking in architectural terms, a vaulted ceiling and raising ceiling refers to a self-supporting arch over the walls and below a roof. If you love to travel, then you have probably noticed vaulted ceilings and raised ceilings in domed spaces and cathedrals like the Pantheon in Rome. Raising ceilings and vaulted ceilings draw the eye upward to create a sense of volume and spaciousness; they add drama to otherwise ordinary rooms.

In hindsight, virtually any house with a sloped roof is able to support a vaulted ceiling. However, it requires an attic space to be present as it is needed to construct the vault. Those with steeper roof pitches are essential for higher vaults, while lower-pitched roofs will only serve shallower vaults.

Any room in your house can be vaulted; it all really just depends on your personal preference. Most homeowners choose vaulted ceilings and raising ceilings for their family room, as this is where it will be most appreciated.
